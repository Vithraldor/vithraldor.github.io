About Me

Engineer, Musician, Community Hero

Hi! My name is Ashley, and I'm currently a third-year student at the University of Toronto majoring in electrical engineering. I am currently specializing in electronics and signal processing, and hope to obtain a certificate in music technology as well.

In my spare time, I dabble in music creation, coding, and hardware projects. I'm also heavily involved in Toronto's local esports scene, having been dubbed a 'community hero' - read more about my contributions here.

I am doing so while gaining valuable experience in working with various electronic components. I also write news articles for The Cannon, one of UofT Engineering's student newspapers.

Outside of school, I work with Toronto Defiant, a professional Overwatch team. I moderate its Discord server and host community events, such as game nights and tournaments.

Side Projects

Hardware

Light-Based Theremin
A two-part analog circuit that resembles a theremin, a musical instrument played by waving your hands. The design consists of an oscillator (square wave generator) and an additional amplifier for master volume control. Currently being adapted for an ECE231 lab at the University of Toronto.

More Information + Design Steps (Schematics included)

The Harmonizer
Hardware project built using an Arduino hooked up to an LCD display and a speaker. Randomly generates a chord progression, geared towards musicians who aim to develop their improvisational skills. This serves as my introduction to Arduino and working with embedded systems.

Devpost (includes video demo and source code)

Software

Mappyboi
Google Maps-like software designed for a school course, developed in a team of three students. Employs OSM data to visualize 19 different cities and various points of interest (like restaurants or parks). Also makes use of Dijkstra's algorithm in order to devise a travel route between two points. Coded in C++.

Source code not available.

Stacker
Final project made for a computer organization course. Designed to run using a DE-SoC1 FPGA, utilizing pushbuttons and a VGA display for I/O. This is a remake of the classic arcade game Stacker, where users must stack blocks moving back-and-forth in order to build a tower up to the top of the screen. Coded in C.
